Research Question

Are there differences in tap water characteristics across regions in South Australia?

To answer this question, the analysis addresses three sub-questions:

  1. How do tap water characteristics vary across operational regions in South Australia?
  2. Which water quality parameters show the greatest variation between regions?
  3. How much variation exists between water supply systems within an operational region, and can regional averages mask these differences?

I chose this question because I am interested in sustainability and environmental systems, particularly how natural resources are managed across different communities. South Australia was selected as the case study because it is one of Australia’s driest states (Department for Environment and Water, 2026a), making water management especially important. After moving from Thailand to Australia, I noticed that drinking tap water directly is much more common in Australia, which made me curious about whether tap water characteristics differ across regions and local water supply systems.

Data Sources and Suitability

Dataset 1: SA Water Quality Performance Results

The primary dataset is the SA Water Quality Performance Results dataset published by SA Water through the South Australian Government Open Data Portal (SA Water, 2026b). It contains drinking water quality information, including water quality parameters, average values, guideline values, and disinfection methods, for SA Water operational regions and water supply systems across metropolitan, regional, and remote communities in South Australia.

This dataset is suitable because it includes both regional and system-level information, allowing comparison of tap water characteristics across South Australia and between individual water supply systems. The reported values are based on SA Water’s routine monitoring program and represent rolling averages over the previous 12 months. As the data are collected through established monitoring and quality assurance processes, the dataset provides a reliable basis for regional comparison.

Australian tap water may contain naturally occurring minerals, treatment chemicals, and trace contaminants depending on the water source and distribution system (Complete Home Filtration, 2024). Tap water characteristics refer to the physical and chemical properties of drinking water that influence its quality, safety, and consumer acceptability. These characteristics can be broadly grouped into mineral composition, aesthetic characteristics (such as taste, appearance, and odour), and water treatment characteristics (National Health and Medical Research Council, 2026).

Australian tap water is regulated under the Australian Drinking Water Guidelines (ADWG), which provide health-based and aesthetic guideline values for drinking water quality parameters (National Health and Medical Research Council, 2026). To represent the main characteristics of tap water, a set of parameters was selected from each category. Together, these parameters provide a broad view of differences in mineral content, water quality, and treatment practices across South Australia.

Why investigate tap water?

South Australia is one of Australia’s driest states (Department for Environment and Water, 2026a), making effective water management particularly important. Although Australian tap water is generally considered safe to drink, drinking water characteristics can vary depending on water sources, mineral composition, and treatment processes. In South Australia, differences in taste are often associated with higher mineral concentrations and chlorine used during water treatment, even when water quality meets Australian Drinking Water Guidelines (ADWG) (Metropolitan Plumbing, 2024).

After moving from Thailand to Australia, one of the things that surprised me was how common it is to drink water directly from the tap. This raised an interesting question: if people across South Australia rely on different water sources and supply systems, do they actually receive water with similar characteristics?

Using publicly available SA Water monitoring data, this blog investigates whether tap water characteristics differ across South Australia, which water quality parameters vary the most between regions, and whether regional averages mask variation between individual water supply systems.

Where does the data come from?

This analysis uses the SA Water Quality Performance Results dataset published by SA Water through the South Australian Government Open Data Portal (SA Water, 2026b), which contains drinking water quality measurements for water supply systems across South Australia. The reported values are rolling 12-month averages collected through SA Water’s routine monitoring program.

To visualise regional patterns, the South Australian Government Regions dataset (Government of South Australia, 2025) was also used. Since government regions do not align directly with SA Water operational regions, an approximate correspondence table was created for mapping.

The analysis focuses on selected water quality parameters representing mineral composition, taste and appearance characteristics, and water treatment characteristics. These parameters provide a manageable way to compare tap water characteristics across South Australia.

What does the data tell us?

How do tap water characteristics vary across regions?

To compare regional tap water characteristics, three representative parameters were selected: Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) for mineral composition, pH for taste and appearance characteristics, and free residual chlorine for water treatment characteristics. According to the Australian Drinking Water Guidelines (ADWG), the aesthetic guideline for TDS is 600 mg/L, the acceptable pH range is 6.5–8.5, and chlorine concentrations up to 5 mg/L are considered acceptable in drinking water (National Health and Medical Research Council, 2026).

These maps (Figure 1, Figure 2, Figure 3) show that all regions recorded values within ADWG guideline ranges, indicating that drinking water quality remains acceptable across South Australia. However, noticeable regional differences exist. TDS showed the largest variation, with the South East and Eyre regions recording higher mineral concentrations than Metropolitan and Outer Metro regions. Higher TDS values indicate greater concentrations of dissolved minerals, which can influence the taste and perceived hardness of tap water (National Health and Medical Research Council, 2026). In contrast, pH varied only slightly between regions, while free residual chlorine showed moderate variation, reflecting differences in water treatment and distribution systems.

These regional patterns suggest that differences in drinking water characteristics are driven more by mineral-related properties than by treatment-related characteristics. To investigate this further, the next section compares variation across all selected water quality parameters to identify which characteristics contribute most to regional differences.

Which water quality parameters vary the most?

To identify which water quality characteristics contribute most to the observed regional differences, the coefficient of variation (CV) was calculated for all selected parameters. Unlike standard deviation, the CV measures variation relative to the average value of each parameter, allowing comparison between variables with different units and scales.

Figure 4 and Table 1 show that turbidity exhibited the greatest regional variation, followed by several mineral-related parameters including magnesium, calcium, alkalinity, sodium, and sulphate. Although TDS showed clear regional differences in Figure 1, several individual mineral parameters varied even more relative to their average values. In contrast, pH showed very little variation across regions, consistent with Figure 2, while free residual chlorine in Figure 3 displayed only moderate variation.

Together, these results suggest that regional differences in drinking water are driven primarily by mineral-related characteristics rather than treatment-related characteristics. However, because the analysis is based on regional averages, it may conceal important differences between individual water supply systems. The next section explores whether regional averages are hiding local variation.

Are regional averages hiding local differences?

Because TDS showed clear regional differences in Figure 1 and is closely related to dissolved mineral content, it was selected for further investigation. The Remote Communities region was chosen because it contains many individual water supply systems and displayed noticeable variation in TDS, allowing local differences to be explored in more detail.

Figure 5 reveals substantial variation between water supply systems. Although the Remote Communities regional average is approximately 360 mg/L, individual systems range from around 30 mg/L in Oak Valley to over 800 mg/L in Watinuma. Several systems exceed the ADWG aesthetic guideline of 600 mg/L, while others remain well below both the guideline and regional average.

This variation suggests that local water supply systems can differ considerably even within the same operational region. Differences in source water and mineral composition may contribute to these patterns. Higher TDS values indicate greater concentrations of dissolved minerals, which may contribute to a stronger mineral taste (Metropolitan Plumbing, 2024). Therefore, regional averages are useful for identifying broad patterns but may not fully represent the experience of individual communities.

So, does tap water differ across South Australia?

This analysis shows that tap water characteristics do vary across South Australia, but the differences are primarily related to mineral composition rather than drinking water safety. While pH remained relatively consistent across regions, larger differences were observed for mineral-related characteristics such as Total Dissolved Solids (TDS), calcium, magnesium, and sulphate.

These differences are likely influenced by the diverse water sources used across South Australia, including River Murray water, reservoirs, groundwater, and desalinated water (Department for Environment and Water, 2026b; Environment Protection Authority South Australia, 2018). However, all regions generally remained within Australian Drinking Water Guidelines (ADWG) ranges (National Health and Medical Research Council, 2026).

The analysis also revealed that regional averages can hide substantial local variation. Within the Remote Communities region, TDS values ranged from around 30 mg/L to over 800 mg/L, demonstrating that water characteristics may differ considerably between individual supply systems.

Overall, the results suggest that perceptions of differences in South Australian tap water are not entirely unfounded. While drinking water generally remains within ADWG guideline ranges, regional differences in mineral composition can influence taste and contribute to different drinking water experiences across communities (Tait, 2025).

What could be explored further?

This analysis focused on selected water quality characteristics using data from a single reporting period. Future work could expand the analysis by:

  • Investigating how tap water characteristics change over time using multiple historical SA Water datasets.
  • Examining whether regional differences in water quality are associated with specific water sources, such as River Murray water, groundwater, reservoirs, or desalinated water.
  • Including additional water quality parameters, such as hardness, aluminium, or nitrate, to provide a more comprehensive assessment of regional water quality.
  • Exploring whether measured water quality characteristics are related to consumer perceptions of tap water taste and drinking water quality.

Some challenges encountered during this project included:

  • The SA Water dataset uses operational regions, while the spatial dataset uses South Australian Government regions. Because no official correspondence table was available, I had to develop an approximate mapping manually.
  • I initially attempted to display all selected water quality parameters using faceted maps. However, large differences in units and value ranges made comparisons difficult and sometimes visually misleading, so I refined the scope to focus on three representative parameters.
  • The dataset does not contain detailed information about water sources for individual supply systems, limiting my ability to explain the exact causes of regional differences.
  • I originally considered analysing changes in water quality over time. However, combining multiple historical SA Water datasets was beyond the scope of this project.

A key skill developed during this project was working with spatial data in R, including joining spatial and tabular datasets, creating centroids for maps labels, and producing maps to investigate regional patterns.

I was particularly proud of solving the mismatch between the two regional classification systems and creating operational region maps that could be linked to the water quality data. This required manually developing a correspondence table because no official mapping was available, and it enabled the regional visualisations that formed a key part of the analysis.

Some limitations of this analysis and potential future improvements include:

  • I used data from a single reporting period, which provides only a snapshot of tap water characteristics. In future, I would like to combine multiple historical SA Water datasets to investigate how water quality changes over time.
  • I focused on a selected set of water quality parameters to keep the analysis manageable. Including additional parameters could provide a more comprehensive assessment of regional water quality.
